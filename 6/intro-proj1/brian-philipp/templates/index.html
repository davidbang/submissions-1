How My Exploration Evolved
I began this project thinking I would simply use the given data, manipulate it,
and add it what I already knew about a few players.
In fact, I learned more than I thought I would.
I made connections between what I knew and my analyzed data.
I learned something new not just about a few players, but about countries and players in general.

Obstacles
When I downloaded the original data, it opened in Adobe.
The only usable format I could convert it to was .txt.
However, the txt file had several issues with that made it difficult to use.
For example, Adobe misalligned the headings of the table and the page number of the Adobe version was in the txt file.
I manually fixed the heading errors and used Python to skip the page numbers and empty lines.

Summary

There are several great tennis players from Spain, so I was not surprised to see Spain in first place.
However, I was surprised by the overall rankings by country.
USA is in fifth, despite having only a few great players (such as John Isner).
In the 2012 Olympics Tennis Finals, Murray (GBR) defeated Federer (SUI).
However, GBR is only in eighth place and SUI is only in seventh.
Also, I was very surprised to see FRA in second, although it made more sense
when I remembered that Tsonga plays for FRA.
I also noticed that there are exactly 100 countries represented in the ATP Rankings.
I'm not sure if this is a coincidence or not.
I learned something new from the second analyzed data table as well:
Grand Slams do not play as significant a role in a player's total points as I thought.
The first fifteen players (the best players) attend more Grand Slam and Masters 1000 tournaments than others
Most of the other players get the majority of their points from other tournaments.
Even within the best fifteen players, only five of them have Grand Slam as their majority.
Four of these five players are the top four players.
